The Simple Shapes of Autonomic Activation

By Scott Goley · Published 2026-06-18

In 1947, a young Kurt Vonnegut submitted a master’s thesis to the anthropology department at the University of Chicago. His thesis proposed a simple, beautiful idea: that stories have shapes, and that these shapes can be plotted on a graph. The horizontal axis represented the chronology of the narrative, from beginning to end. The vertical axis represented the fortune of the protagonist, ranging from "Good Fortune" at the top to "Ill Fortune" at the bottom.

Vonnegut plotted the universal architectures of human narrative: the "Man in a Hole" curve (a character starts well, falls into trouble, and climbs back out), the "Boy Meets Girl" curve (bliss, loss, and redemption), and "Cinderella" (a steady rise, a midnight crash, and a final, permanent ascent). The university's anthropology faculty promptly rejected the thesis, declaring it unscientific. Decades later, researchers utilizing computational natural language processing analyzed thousands of novels and proved Vonnegut entirely correct. The human mind is structurally organized to interpret meaning through these recurring shapes of rising and falling fortune.

But there is another, deeper set of curves that dictates our actual, lived experience of reality. They do not live on the pages of novels; they are written directly into our tissues. They are the shapes of Autonomic Activation.

If we translate Vonnegut's narrative axes into the language of biophysics, we find a remarkably similar graphing opportunity. On the horizontal axis, we plot Time—from the moment our eyelids crack open in the morning to the moment we fall into unconsciousness at night. On the vertical axis, we plot Autonomic Activation.

At the very bottom of the vertical axis lies the peak of Parasympathetic Dominance—ventral vagal ease, cellular restoration, low heart rate, high heart rate variability (HRV), and circadian safety. At the very top of the vertical axis lies the peak of Sympathetic Dominance—high heart rate, energy mobilization, thoracic shallow breathing, catecholamine floods (adrenaline and noradrenaline), cortisol spikes, and the threat-scanning vigilance of fight-or-flight.

Just as a society can be understood by the shapes of the stories it tells, a historical era—and a person's socioeconomic class—can be diagnosed by the autonomic shape of their average day. In our hyper-capitalist, screen-saturated, CO₂-tightened modern world, the autonomic curve has undergone a catastrophic distortion. We have transitioned from the smooth, predictable, low-amplitude hills of our evolutionary ancestors to the jagged, high-frequency, volatile storms of somatic poverty.

This analysis traces the history of the autonomic day, plots the curves of seven distinct historical and class profiles, explores the biophysics of modern somatic extraction, and outlines the paths to reclaiming the autonomic commons.

1. Biophysical Principles of the Autonomic Curve

To understand the shapes of these curves, we must first map the physiological forces that pull the autonomic line upward or draw it downward.

The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) is the body's primary mechanism for maintaining internal homeostasis in response to external demands. It operates along a spectrum governed by two primary branches:

  1. The Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) [Activation]: Governed by the sympathetic chain ganglia and the adrenal medulla, this branch prepares the organism for physical action or survival. When active, it elevates heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration rate, contracts peripheral blood vessels, and shuts down non-essential somatic processes like digestion, immune function, and cellular repair. It is fueled by catecholamines (adrenaline/noradrenaline) and the glucocorticoid cortisol.
  2. The Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS) [Deactivation]: Governed primarily by the vagus nerve (Cranial Nerve X), this branch facilitates energy conservation, tissue growth, and digestion. It slows the heart rate, lowers blood pressure, stimulates gastrointestinal motility, and modulates inflammation via the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway. Its primary neurotransmitter is acetylcholine.

Autonomic health is not the absence of sympathetic activation; it is the presence of autonomic resilience. A healthy nervous system acts like a flexible pendulum, rapidly up-regulating to meet a cognitive or physical challenge, and immediately down-regulating into ventral vagal safety once the challenge is resolved. This resilience is measured via Heart Rate Variability (HRV)—the millisecond-level variation between successive heartbeats. High HRV indicates a highly responsive, balanced autonomic system; low HRV indicates a rigid system locked in sympathetic dominance or dorsal vagal collapse.

Three biological axes dictate the daily curve:

  • The Circadian Light-Endocrine Axis: The contrast between morning high-lux sunlight (which triggers the Cortisol Awakening Response or CAR) and evening darkness (which triggers the melatonin surge). A healthy day requires a high-amplitude contrast between day and night.
  • The Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) Respiration Axis: The balance of O₂ and CO₂ in the blood. Shallow, rapid breathing (screen apnea) prevents the offloading of CO₂, leading to systemic acidosis. The brainstem monitors blood pH; elevated CO₂ triggers a chemoreceptor-driven threat alarm in the amygdala, generating "soft" anxiety without an obvious cognitive cause.
  • The Visual Field Axis: Focal, narrow vision (looking at screens) biologically activates sympathetic threat-scanning. Dilated, peripheral vision (horizon gazing) relaxes extraocular muscles and sends a direct signal to the brainstem to disengage sympathetic vigilance.

When we plot the daily autonomic curves of human beings across historical eras and class structures, we see a clear progression. We have moved from a unified, monophasic rhythm aligned with the solar cycle to a fragmented, volatile, and highly extracted somatic profile.

The ancient agricultural worker lived in homeostatic alignment with the natural diurnal cycle. Lacking artificial light or mechanical clocks, their day was anchored by the sun.

  • Morning (Waking): As the sun rises, natural high-lux blue light enters the eyes, stimulating the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) to suppress melatonin and trigger a robust, healthy Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR). Respiration is deep and nasal. Autonomic activation rises in a gentle, linear slope as they begin physical labor.
  • Midday (Mobilization & Rest): Physical labor is characterized by a moderate, steady sympathetic arousal. At midday, they rest to eat. The meal follows ancient, flat-curve glycemic sequencing: starting with fiber-rich wild greens or bitter herbs, followed by slow-burning complex starches and fats. This prevents any high-amplitude insulin spike. Following the meal, they engage in deambulatio (postprandial walking) or rest in Vajrasana (the yogic thunderbolt pose, redirecting blood flow to the digestive tract). The sympathetic line dips into a gentle, restorative parasympathetic valley.
  • Evening (Wind-down): As dusk falls, light levels drop to zero. The pineal gland immediately releases melatonin, lowering core body temperature and raising parasympathetic tone.
  • Night (Sleep): They drop into deep, slow-wave sleep. With no late-night cognitive stimulation or screen exposure, their brain's glymphatic system clears metabolic waste. Vagal tone is high, resting HRV is maximized, and cellular repair is complete.
  • Autonomic Signature: A wide, smooth, monophasic hill. The system rises once for action and sinks once for deep rest.

The Industrial Revolution shattered the solar alignment of the human body, introducing shift work, the factory floor, and the mechanical clock.

  • Morning (Circadian Shock): Woken abruptly in the dark by a mechanical whistle or steam alarm. The body undergoes a sudden, defensive sympathetic spike without the preparation of natural morning light.
  • Day (Somatic Dissociation): The worker enters the factory—a sensory nightmare of high-decibel clatter (sub-bass vibrations simulating tissue impact), toxic coal fumes, and high-density crowds. They execute repetitive physical motions for 12 to 16 hours. To survive, they must ignore interoceptive feedback (pain, fatigue, hunger), establishing a state of chronic somatic dissociation. The autonomic line is pinned to a flat, high sympathetic plateau.
  • Evening (Chemical Crash): The worker lacks the somatic skills or environments to down-regulate. To escape the sympathetic pressure cooker, they turn to the first mass-marketed chemical down-regulator: cheap, industrial alcohol (gin or whiskey). This acts as a GABA agonist, blunting their sensory receptors and forcing the sympathetic line downward.
  • Night (Fragile Rest): Sleep is brief, crowded, and disrupted by the toxic environment and alcohol-induced sleep fragmentation (rebound glutamate spikes). The glymphatic system remains under-active.
  • Autonomic Signature: A harsh boxcar shape. A vertical ascent into sympathetic alarm, a flat, grueling plateau, and a sudden, chemical drop ending in high-frequency nocturnal stress.

4. The Biophysics of Somatic Extraction

To understand how modern hyper-capitalism capitalizes on these curves, we must look at the specific biological loops it exploits. In the modern economy, somatic dysregulation is not an accident; it is a business model.

A fully regulated, somatically sovereign individual is fundamentally unprofitable. If you know how to drop your shoulders, expand your gaze, slow your breath, stabilize your glucose curve, and co-regulate with your community for free, you do not need to buy what the market is selling. You do not need the subscription mindfulness app, the premium recovery beverage, the morning stimulant, the evening sedative, the processed comfort food, or the high-end spa treatment.

Somatic extraction operates as a closed-loop system:

         │                                                  (Lack of Somatic Skills)
         │                                                            │
         │                                                            ▼
[Purchase Corporate Proxies] <──(Sells Passive Relief)── [Somatic Distress]
  1. The Construction of Sympathetic Stress: The market constructs high-stress, high-friction environments—open offices, high-density traffic commutes, algorithmic social media feeds, and poor indoor air quality. These inputs drive the customer's autonomic nervous system upward into chronic sympathetic dominance.
  2. The Systematic Decimation of Somatic Skills: Modern education and culture offer no instruction in mechanical self-regulation. We are not taught how to use the breath to change blood chemistry, how to use the eyes to disengage the amygdala, or how to discharge stress hormones through motor release.
  3. The Sale of Commodified Proxies: Because the individual cannot manually regulate their nervous system, their chronic sympathetic activation turns into "soft" anxiety and somatic distress. The market then steps in to sell commodified, passive parasympathetic proxies to temporarily relieve the very anxiety it created:
  • Instead of deep breathing, it sells mindfulness app subscriptions.
  • Instead of horizon gazing, it sells blue-light blocking glasses.
  • Instead of natural co-regulation, it sells digital adult content or virtual intimacy.
  • Instead of metabolic stability, it sells high-end diet supplements and weight-loss drugs.
  • Instead of circadian wind-downs, it sells weighted blankets and sleep tracking wearables.

The customer is kept locked in a cycle of physiological debt, paying the market to temporarily buffer the biological toll of living in a capitalist environment.

5. Somatic Inequality, AirPods, and Boomer Insulation

One of the most insidious developments of the late 2010s and 2020s is the emergence of auditory pollution and attention capture via headphones (AirPods).

Historically, walking outside was a natural, non-commodified parasympathetic reset. When a human walks through a natural environment, their eyes naturally engage in lateral optic flow—the visual movement of trees, buildings, and ground passing by on the sides. This lateral visual movement, combined with horizon gazing, physically relaxes the extraocular muscles. This sends a direct, evolutionary signal to the brainstem that the organism is moving forward through space and that the environment is safe, disengaging the sympathetic threat-scan.

Visual Focus   ──► Constricted (Screens)  ──► Sympathetic Threat Scan
Lateral Flow   ──► Dilation (Horizon)     ──► Parasympathetic Safety

However, the modern worker rarely walks in silence. The moment they step outside, they place AirPods in their ears. They do not look at the horizon; they look at their phone to select a podcast, queue a music playlist, or call a colleague.

By keeping the auditory cortex occupied with complex cognitive information (e.g., a fast-paced conversation, a political podcast, high-beat music), the prefrontal cortex is kept in an active processing state. The brain's Default Mode Network (DMN)—which requires silence and cognitive idleness to organize thoughts, process emotions, and lower autonomic tone—is never allowed to engage.

Further, because they are scrolling or checking notifications while walking, their visual field remains constricted to a narrow, 12-inch screen. The lateral optic flow is neutralized. What should have been a parasympathetic deactivation valley becomes another high-frequency sympathetic loading event.

This attention capture highlights a deep generational divide: Boomer Insulation versus Millennial/Gen Z Somatic Poverty.

Individuals born before the mid-1980s spent their formative 30 to 50 years in a pre-smartphone environment. They developed deeply ingrained, physical habits of autonomic navigation:

  • They sat in traffic or stood in lines in silence, allowing their minds to wander and their default mode networks to engage naturally.
  • They ate meals without looking at a screen, allowing the cephalic phase of digestion to operate properly.
  • They resolved boredom through daydreams, visual relaxation, and natural co-regulation.

These habits became part of their muscular and neurological structure. Even when they adopt modern smartphones, this underlying "somatic insulation" protects them. They can put their phone down and return to a stable, low-activation baseline because their nervous system remembers the way back.

In contrast, younger generations (Millennials and Gen Z) have known nothing but continuous algorithmic intervention. Their attention has been programmatically traded since childhood. Lacking the developmental template of a silent, un-extracted day, they have no muscle memory of ventral vagal safety. When they try to sit in silence, their brain interprets the lack of sensory input as an emergency, triggering a sympathetic alarm. They are "somatic orphans," completely dependent on external, commodified inputs to regulate their internal states.

6. Reclaiming the Autonomic Commons: Active vs. Passive Downregulation

To exit this loop of somatic extraction, we must transition from passive somatic consumption to mechanical somatic sovereignty.

This requires recognizing the difference between Passive Downregulation (which builds dependency and extracts profit) and Active Downregulation (which builds autonomic resilience and reclaims sovereignty).

Passive (Extractive):  Consuming Alcohol ──► Blunts GABA ──► Next-day Stress Rebound
Active (Sovereign):   Resonant Breath  ──► Stimulates VN ──► Long-term HRV Resilience

The Taxonomy of Downregulation Levers

1. Respiration Levers (The Primary Vagal Brake)

  • Active (Sovereign):
  • The Physiological Sigh: Double inhale through the nose (full breath + sharp sniff to inflate collapsed lung sacs), followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth. Repeating this 3 times immediately offloads the CO₂ buildup that triggers brainstem panic, slowing the heart rate via the vagus nerve's release of acetylcholine.
  • Coherence Breathing: Breathing at a rate of 5 to 6 breaths per minute (typically a 5-second inhale and 5-second exhale). This synchronizes cardiac, respiratory, and blood pressure rhythms (cardiovascular resonance), maximizing HRV and building long-term vagal tone.
  • Left-Nostril Breathing (Chandra Bhedana): Inhaling through the left nostril and expressing through the right, targeting the right cerebral hemisphere to promote parasympathetic dominance.
  • Passive (Extractive):
  • Mindfulness Subscriptions: Paying a tech corporation for a voice to guide your breathing through a device that is simultaneously serving you notifications.

2. Visual Levers (The Ocular-Vagal Reset)

  • Active (Sovereign):
  • Horizon Gazing: Looking up and dilating your visual field to take in your entire peripheral field. This relaxes extraocular muscles, signaling the superior colliculus in the brainstem to disengage sympathetic vigilance.
  • Circadian Sunlight Anchoring: Getting 5 to 10 minutes of direct, morning light in the eyes to set the SCN, ensuring a clean, high-amplitude day-night cortisol-melatonin contrast.
  • Passive (Extractive):
  • Blue-Light Glasses & Sleep Goggles: Purchasing physical products to filter out the artificial environment, rather than managing your light exposure directly.

3. Motor & Somatic Levers (Proprioceptive Safety)

  • Active (Sovereign):
  • Vigorous Somatic Shaking: Bouncing and shaking the limbs and torso for 2 minutes when in a state of high sympathetic arousal. This discharges cumulative stress hormones, completing the evolutionary stress cycle.
  • Vocal Resonant Toning: Exhaling while making a low-pitched, resonant vocal vibration (such as a deep "Voo" sound). The physical vibration stimulates the pharyngeal and laryngeal branches of the vagus nerve in the throat, slowing the pulse and shifting the autonomic state toward calm.
  • Ocular-Vagal Reset: Interlocking the hands behind the skull and looking to the far right/left with the eyes until a spontaneous yawn, sigh, or swallow occurs, signaling suboccipital relaxation and vagal activation.
  • Passive (Extractive):
  • Boutique Fitness & High-End Spas: Paying high fees to enter sensory deprivation tanks, weighted massage rooms, or high-intensity classes to force a physical downregulation that you cannot access at home.

4. Metabolic & Dietary Levers (Glycemic Stability)

  • Active (Sovereign):
  • Fiber-First Sequencing: Meal sequencing that begins with fiber (greens/vegetables), followed by protein and fats, and ends with carbohydrates. This creates a viscous mesh in the small intestine, slowing glucose absorption and flattening the glycemic curve.
  • Postprandial Walking: Walking for 10 to 15 minutes immediately after a meal to draw glucose directly into skeletal muscles, preventing high-amplitude insulin spikes and subsequent adrenaline crashes.
  • Passive (Extractive):
  • Alcohol & Simple Sugars: Using chemical depressants or fast sugar spikes to force immediate, short-term downregulation, paid for with interest during the next-day rebound stress phase.

By choosing active, mechanical practices over passive, commodified proxies, we withdraw our bodies from the somatic extraction machine. Autonomic regulation is not a luxury product; it is a biological birthright. Reclaiming the shape of our day is the first, most fundamental act of somatic sovereignty.
