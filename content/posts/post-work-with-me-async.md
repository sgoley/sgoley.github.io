# Work with me async

The best async collaboration starts with a useful artifact, not a meeting request.
If you are exploring whether I can help, the fastest path is to leave enough
context that I can reason about the problem, compare it against prior work, and
respond with specific next steps.

## What to leave

- **Goal:** the outcome you want, not just the tool or task you have in mind.
- **Current state:** what exists today, what works, and what is failing.
- **Constraints:** timeline, access limits, data sensitivity, budget, team shape, and non-negotiable technical choices.
- **Evidence:** links, repositories, screenshots, schemas, logs, examples, previous attempts, decisions already made, and anything that narrows ambiguity.
- **Success criteria:** how we would know the work is done and useful.

## Strong async requests

Strong requests usually sound like:

- "Here is the system, here is the failure mode, here are the examples, and here is what a good answer would let us decide."
- "Here is a repo or document set. Please identify the risky assumptions before we commit to this direction."
- "Here is a rough idea. Please turn it into an implementation brief with open questions and a first vertical slice."

Weak requests usually hide the important part:

- "Can you look at this?"
- "Can we hop on a call?"
- "What would you do with AI here?"

## How the handoff packet helps

The homepage workbench is designed to create durable residue from the interaction:
the prompt, the answer, the source links, the context you supplied, and the
permission boundary for reuse. That packet gives me something concrete to review,
quote, transform into future content, or use as seed material for unrelated
projects.

Do not include secrets, credentials, private customer data, or confidential
material. If the sensitive details matter, describe the shape of the problem and
the redactions you made.

## What I am especially useful for

- Data and analytics engineering systems.
- dbt, SQL, Airflow, Snowflake, Postgres, and pipeline reliability.
- Developer tools, automation, and small agents that make messy work repeatable.
- Turning scattered notes into a plan, PRD, implementation brief, or issue set.
- Reviewing architecture and identifying the assumptions likely to break first.
